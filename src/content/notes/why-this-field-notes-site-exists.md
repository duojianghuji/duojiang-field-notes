There is a practical reason to keep a field-notes site separate from a research site.

The research site needs to be clear, stable, and easy for collaborators to scan. The field-notes site can be slower, more visual, and more associative. It can hold the material that supports a way of seeing without needing to justify itself as a formal output.

That difference matters. When everything is forced into one site, the scientific work can become harder to parse and the personal work can become too cautious. Two sites create two distinct reading modes.

The connection between them is still real: attention. One site tracks scientific questions; the other tracks places, images, and references that keep attention alive.
