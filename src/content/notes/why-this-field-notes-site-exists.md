There is a practical reason to keep two separate sites.

The research site is where the peer-reviewed work lives. It's organized, professional, and built for collaborators to navigate quickly. It has to be. That's where credibility lives.

But the work that feeds that research—the actual practice of paying attention—needs a different kind of home. This site is where the observations go before they're ready to be formal. A photograph from a field site that hasn't yet become data. A reading note that might connect three different things. A travel entry about how a landscape changes the way you think about water movement.

None of this is publishable in a journal. None of it needs to be. But without it, the research work becomes disconnected from the thing that started it: curiosity about how plants actually live.

Two sites mean two different kinds of writing are possible. The research site asks: what is true? This site asks: what changed the way I look?

The connection between them runs deeper than it appears. Every entry here is practice in attention. And all the best research questions come from people who know how to pay attention.
